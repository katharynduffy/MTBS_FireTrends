Description:
Abstract:
The Monitoring Trends in Burn Severity (MTBS) Program assesses the frequency, extent, and magnitude (size and severity) of all large fires (includes wildfire, wildland fire use, and prescribed fire) in the conterminous United States (CONUS), Alaska, Hawaii, and Puerto Rico for the period 1984 and beyond. All fires reported as greater than 1,000 acres in the western U.S. and greater than 500 acres in the eastern U.S. are mapped across all ownerships. MTBS produces a series of geospatial and tabular data for analysis at a range of spatial, temporal, and thematic scales and are intended to meet a variety of information needs that require consistent data about fire effects through space and time. This map layer is a thematic raster image of MTBS burn severity classes for all inventoried and mappable fires occurring in Arizona during calendar year 2003.
Purpose:
The primary objective of MTBS is to provide consistent burn severity data and fire perimeters for all large fires within the United States. The datasets are generated using a standardized process and produced at a resolution of 30 meters. The program provides data and products to four primary user groups including national policy-makers, field management units, other federal programs such as LANDFIRE and academic and agency research entities. These groups benefit from the availability of GIS data produced in multiple formats, as well as easy-to-read published maps.

Process_Description:
For a detailed definition and discussion on the processing and production of MTBS geospatial data, please refer to <http://www.mtbs.gov>. Otherwise a synopsis of the production of this layer is provided below.

MTBS burn severity class data are derived from Landsat Thematic Mapper data. A pre-fire scene and a post-fire scene are analyzed to create a Differenced Normalized Burn Ratio (dNBR) image. The continuous dNBR image portrays the variations of burn severity within the fire. In exceptional circumstances, a suitable pre-fire image may not be available to generate a dNBR . Consequently, in these circumstances, analysis and assessment is conducted using the post-fire NBR image only.

The MTBS program utilizes Landsat images produced by two USGS product generations sytems - the National Landsat Archive Production System (NLAPS) and the Level 1 Product Generation System (LPGS). NLAPS imagery are terrain corrected and geometrically rectified to an Albers Conical Equal Area map projection. The NLAPS images are further processed to convert bands 1-5 and 7 to at-satellite-reflectance. LPGS imagery are terrain corrected, geometrically rectified to Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) projection, converted to at-satellite-reflectance, and subsequently reprojected to Albers Conical Equal Area map projection.

For each mapped fire, the Normalized Burn Ratio (NBR) is computed for each date of imagery using the following formula:

((Band 4 - Band 7) / (Band 4 + Band 7)) x 1000 = NBR

The differenced NBR is computed by subtracting the post-fire NBR from the pre-fire NBR:

PreNBR - PostNBR = dNBR

The perimeter of the fire is delineated using the dNBR and post-fire reflectance image. Continuous dNBR data are then thresholded into burn severity classes with higher dNBR values correlated to higher burn severities and lower dNBR values relating to less severe burn severites. The dNBR image for each fire is evaluated to determine the appropriate dNBR threshold to discriminate between burned and unburned areas, and low, modererate and high severity areas. For some fires, an increased response in vegetation productivity may result (areas of very low (negative) dNBR values) and are identified as areas of "Increased Greenness". Additionally, non-land surface features or areas occluded by obscurations in the pre or post-fire Landsat image are masked out (i.e. large water bodies, clouds, shadows, smoke, snow, etc.) and identified as "Non-Processing Areas".

The completed thresholded burn severity images for each each year are mosaicked to create a seamless thresholded burn severity image. The entire burn severity classification for fires that intersect the specified geographic region are maintained in the mosaic. The mosaicking process utilizes a maximum overlap function where pixels in overlapping areas of two or more input images will result in the assignment of the maximum pixel value. This situation is very rare and would potentially only happen in a slight overlap area of adjacent fires occurring in the same year.

Overview_Description:
Entity_and_Attribute_Overview:
MTBS thematic burn severity classes are thresholded from continuous dNBR images (or NBR images in certain circumstances) on a fire by fire basis. Thresholds are based on interpretation by MTBS analysts in the context of available ancillary data. Determination of thresholds are also based on established relationships between composite burn index (CBI) ground plot data and dNBR values where available.
